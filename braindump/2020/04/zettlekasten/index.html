Zettelkasten note taking

  • Write notes not in terms of what they mean but in terms of what you want them to mean.
  • Don’t multitask.
  • No writing notes on the edges of book, underlining and so on since these notes, it is very hard to collect.
  • Start with random notes(fleeting notes) ==> Turn them to literature notes ==> Turn them to permanent notes. In process of organizing put only notes that matter.
  • Keep notes simple as cards. One card can’t have too much of information.
  • Routinize all habits that take away attention: brushing, clothes( Be like Steve Jobs wearing single clothes )
  • Use the Zeigarnik effect to your advantage. If you want something to stop intruding your mind, write it down and promise yourself that you’ll “deal with it later”. If you want to keep pondering something (perhaps a problem you want to solve), don’t write it down, and go for a walk with that problem on your mind.How to take smart notes (Ahrens, 2017) - LessWrong 2.0
  • Literature Notes: Content + Bibliographic information (Where the text is)
  • Linking between notes:
    • Index –> Entry point note
      • Index: List of tags and keywords with links
      • Tag:
        • A topic you care about
        • Linked to a few notes(at most 2)
      • Entry point note:
        • Upto 25 in length
        • Often to give overview to the topics
      • Link types: Sequential and Horizontal
        • Sequential links clarify where to go and stream of thought you can easily follow along.
        • Sequential are also horizontal: just named sequential
    • Note <–> Note
    • Note –> Note
  • Don’t group brainstorm: (Research : (Mullen, Brian, Craig Johnson, and Eduardo Salas, 1991, Productivity Loss in Brainstorming Groups: A Meta-Analytic Integration).)
  • Slow multitaking (Luhmann)
    • When I am stuck for one moment, I leave it and do something else… I always work on different manuscripts at the same time. With this method, to work on different things simultaneously, I never encounter any mental blockages.
  • Collector’s fallacy: You go on collecting more and more information to never use it and see it in life again.
    • Happened with me for all these years. I don’t even remember what I read but I read too much and wrote notes so much. They are lost in the fabric of spacetime.
  • Folder based approach:
    • Hard to draw connections
    • So comes tag based approach: Categories don’t have to be mutually exclusive now.
    • Based on a tag you are focusing on: it spans different folders: streamlines ideas in a different way than the other tag.
    • But with thousand of notes per tag, again it’s a clutter.
    • Now comes the idea of linking between notes: this creates a web of ideas.
    • You have a question and with that question in mind you dive into your Zettelkasten, moving from one idea to another by following links among notes. Since it contains so many ideas, which you’ve been collecting over a time span of years, you’ve forgotten a huge chunk of them. The Zettelkasten is bursting with ideas that you added years ago and no longer remember. Thus, if you explore it with a question in mind, the Zettelkasten will provide answers in surprising ways. Zettelkasten — How One German Scholar Was So Freakishly Productive
  • Principles:
    • Atomicity: Note must have only one idea
    • Autonomy: Note must be comprehensible on it’s own: Moved, processed, separated or concatenated without relevance to it’s neighbors.
    • Linking: Always link your notes
      • A note not linked will be forgotten.
    • Own words: Don’t C-c C-v.
    • References: To know where the idea came from
    • Fuck structure: Put notes as you want it
    • Connection notes: you see one note talking secretly to the other, you marry them with a link.
    • Outline notes: As ideas merge into theme, make outline notes: creating a narrative, argument or a book.
    • Never delete: Just link to new notes to say what’s wrong with old ones.
    • Add infinitely: Don’t say your storage got all used up. Buy a NAS or a new storage unit or invent one if you haven’t enough. ;)
  • Where possible or necessary (i.e., if an idea can be considered as “start” of a new note sequence, but does fit into the current linear argumentation), branch off a new idea-path.
  • Check if similar topic or “story line” has been written down elsewhere; if yes, add manual links / references
  • Identify relevant notes that function as “starting” point and tag these notes
